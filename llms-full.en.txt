> Digital memorial of Ihor "Slon" Utyuzh (April 18, 1996 — December 7, 2023) — defender of Ukraine, UAV operator of the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment, participant of the Revolution of Dignity and the Anti-Terrorist Operation. ## Key facts about the person

- **Full name:** Utyuzh Ihor Olehovych
- **Call sign:** "Slon" (Ukrainian for "Elephant")
- **Born:** April 18, 1996, Kyiv
- **Killed in action:** December 7, 2023, near the village of Verbove, Zaporizhzhia Oblast
- **Unit:** 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment of the Air Assault Forces of the Armed Forces of Ukraine
- **Military specialty:** UAV operator
- **Education:** Kyiv Polytechnic Institute
- **Height:** 1.95 m (6 ft 5 in)
- **Member of the 24th Hundred of the Maidan Self-Defense** (2013–2014), severely wounded on February 18, 2014, in Mariinskyi Park
- **Veteran of the Anti-Terrorist Operation** (since 2016)
- **Fiancée:** Yevheniia Shkliaruk
- **Parents:** Oleh and Nina Utyuzh
- **Circumstances of death:** killed while shielding his brothers-in-arms from an enemy FPV drone during a combat mission as a UAV operator

## Ihor "Slon" Utyuzh

**April 18, 1996 — December 7, 2023**

Defender of Ukraine, soldier of the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment, participant of the Revolution of Dignity. A man who twice made the choice to go first — at 17 to the Maidan, and at 25 to the full-scale war.

> "If my life was saved — then I am needed for something greater."

> "Memory is a light that does not fade."

## Who he was to us

A short story of Ihor's life, character, and path — a man who knew how to stand beside others and how to move forward.

**Childhood and character.** From an early age Ihor was active, energetic, and curious. His parents remember him as a boy who "always found something to do" and had a natural persistence. He practiced martial arts and rowing, loved sports and people. His ability to connect with others was almost mystical — a few words, a smile, and he was already "one of their own" among strangers.

**Life before the war.** Before the full-scale invasion, Ihor lived an ordinary young man's life: studies, friends, travel, football, chess, plans for the future. He loved his city, loved national team matches, loved simple joys. He dreamed of one day going to Africa to protect elephants. Elephants were his childhood love, from which his call sign later grew.

**Revolution of Dignity.** At 17, Ihor made the choice that defined the rest of his life. He became part of the 24th Hundred of the Maidan Self-Defense, stood watch at the barricades, helped pull out the wounded. On February 18, 2014, in Mariinskyi Park, he received a severe wound — part of his skull was crushed. Doctors saved his life. Then he said the phrase that became prophetic: "If my life was saved — then I am needed for something greater."

## The warrior's path

From Maidan to the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment. The key milestones of Ihor's military journey.

**2014 — 2016. First war. ATO.** Despite the severe injury at Maidan, Ihor went through rehabilitation and joined the Anti-Terrorist Operation. He received the medal "For Sacrifice and Love for Ukraine." He came back from the front, but the war would not let him go.

**February 24, 2022. The return.** Before the full-scale invasion, Ihor told his family: "If it begins — I will go." On February 24, 2022, he was abroad — and returned to Ukraine by the first possible routes. He enlisted in the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment. He became a UAV operator.

**2022 — 2023. Kyiv region, Kharkiv region, the South.** He took part in the liberation of the Kyiv and Kharkiv regions. Later — the Zaporizhzhia and Kherson directions. He worked in difficult sectors, supported his brothers-in-arms, never left his own behind. In December 2023, while carrying out a combat mission, his position was attacked by an enemy FPV drone. Ihor was killed shielding his brothers-in-arms — just as he lived: standing beside them, to the end, without fear and without a step back.

## Symbols of his path

**Elephant (Slon).** A call sign born from a childhood love that became a sign of strength, endurance, and the ability to take a blow. "Slon" — the one who moves forward when others stop.

**Shield.** Protection of brothers-in-arms, family, home. What Ihor was for the people around him — both in Mariinskyi Park and in the trenches of the Air Assault Forces.

**Ukraine.** What he chose twice — at 17 and at 25. What he lived for and gave his life for.

## Words that remain

> He was a whole universe for us. A true patriot, a faithful friend and brother-in-arms, with a great heart open to all. In any circumstances, he encouraged others and repeated that everything would be all right. That's how it was at the Maidan, when he was 17. That's how it was later.
> — Mother

> His main wish was our victory. After it, he planned to travel a lot — arranging his routes so he could make it to football matches. He dreamed of a house in the mountains with breathtaking views, and of a daughter — definitely first. He had a goal of creating a charitable foundation, which he would have called "White Elephant."
> — Fiancée

> "Slon" always wanted to be at the front. The commander called him to headquarters — he refused: "I want to enter the cities and plant the flags." And that's what he did.
> — Brother-in-arms, 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment

> A man who knew exactly what his values were and held to them. Sturdy, with broad shoulders you could hide behind. "One of our own" from the first word — and forever.
> — Friend

# Biography

URL: https://slon.life/biography

A memoir text in memory of soldier Ihor Utyuzh, who fell on December 7, 2023. The material is written based on the accounts of Ihor's family: his parents — Oleh and Nina Utyuzh — and his fiancée Yevheniia Shkliaruk.

## Chapter One. One of our own

In his childhood, Ihor was restless and active. A bit of a rascal (in the good sense) with burning eyes and a belief that nothing was impossible. He always sought adventures, and from his early years he forged his character — his schedule filled with karate, then jiu-jitsu, then competitive rowing.

With age, his activity gained an iron character and stubbornness. And a love for people. Ihor could be unofficially called a master of the art of making connections. In his case it was indeed an art: a few words, a smile — and Ihor was already "one of our own," a good companion you could go through fire and water with.

After school, Ihor independently entered the state-funded program at Kyiv Polytechnic Institute. And in his very first year, when he was 17, a turning point came — the Revolution of Dignity began. It shaped Ihor as a citizen of the country and crystallized his values.

> They were drawn there. Sometimes they skipped classes, or right after classes they went to the Maidan with the guys. And after the beating of the students, Ihor found his place there. Already at 17, still essentially a child, he clearly understood that he wanted to live in a free, independent country moving in a pro-European direction, one that would tolerate no manifestations of dictatorship.
> — Nina, Ihor's mother

Ihor was part of the 24th Hundred of the Maidan defenders — and even then he learned what it means to fight for one's own. He was not yet 18 when the Revolution of Dignity became part of his life: he patrolled the Maidan, stood at the barricades, took part in clashes with "titushky" and the Berkut riot police, pulled people out of the hands of the security forces.

> They didn't sleep for several days at a time, and if they managed to rest a bit, it was right there — in tents by the fire or on the floor inside the City Hall building. They were still very young when they had to stand, unarmed and unprotected, against Berkut riot police armed to the teeth and trained "titushky" with rebar. All they had was zeal, courage, and homemade Molotov cocktails. In those rare moments when Ihor broke away to come home, he arrived utterly exhausted and completely black with smoke.
> — Nina, mother

Ihor went through the fierce days on Hrushevskoho Street in the front lines — he was hit several times with rubber bullets and was poisoned by gas. His parents remember the Maidan events with fear and pride: during the standoffs on Hrushevskoho, a fire broke out in one of the shops. On his own initiative, Ihor took up the Ukrainian flag and walked to negotiate with the security forces to let the fire trucks through. A truce had been declared at the barricades for that moment. Brave, persuasive, and so young then, Ihor came back with the flag in his hands, with the firefighters — and with his first victory.

The events of 2014 developed in Ihor a heightened sense of justice and loyalty to his principles. From then on these traits would be his compass and the foundation of every choice he made in life.

> As a child, he was a rascal, so his actions were varied. But from his childhood we tried to instill certain frameworks of behavior in him — showing him examples from books and films. But Ihor and frameworks — these are incompatible. With age, Ihor came to his own framework: conscience, faith in truth, and a sharp sense of justice.
> — Oleh, Ihor's father

## Chapter Two. "If my life was saved — then I am needed for something greater"

Stubbornness, honesty, bravery, persistence — these are the traits that first come to mind when we remember Ihor. He was, above all, a Human Being. Principled, deep, dedicated. That is why he was first everywhere, moving toward danger rather than running from it — at the Maidan in 2014, during the full-scale invasion in 2022. Eight years lie between those two events. But the virtues that drove Ihor remained unchanged.

During the Revolution of Dignity, on February 18, 2014, Ihor received a severe wound near Mariinskyi Park. It happened during a clash between unarmed people and the Berkut. For a long time medical aid was not provided to him — the attackers kept him at the site of the beating, in a state between life and death. Iryna Herashchenko and her colleagues managed to get Ihor to a hospital.

Doctors performed several emergency operations: Ihor was in critical condition — part of his skull was crushed. Eventually a titanium plate had to be installed, and he went through a long course of rehabilitation. Then, after recovery, Ihor said to his parents the phrase that defined everything afterward:

> If my life was saved — then I am needed for something greater.

> If he had not been beaten then, and had not been in the hospital during the days of the tragic mass shootings on the Maidan, we don't know whether Ihor would have remained alive or whether he would have taken part in the Great War he later joined…
> — Parents

Despite the serious injury sustained at the Maidan, within a few years Ihor joined the Anti-Terrorist Operation: he underwent special training and performed defense tasks on the second line. In 2016, at the age of 20, he received the medal "For Sacrifice and Love for Ukraine."

## Chapter Three. "The war has begun! I'm coming home!"

Three or four months before the full-scale invasion, Ihor sensed the approach of a great struggle. And already then he knew this struggle would be his own. So back in the autumn of 2021, he told his loved ones: "If this happens, I will be there, I will go." Ihor left no room for doubt or hesitation. As with all his decisions in life, his desire to defend the country was unwavering.

Ihor met the news of the Russian full-scale invasion while celebrating the anniversary of meeting his girlfriend in Budapest. The morning of February 24 began with two phrases: *The war has begun! I'm coming home!*

> He called more than 20 transport companies. But he received only refusals. No one wanted to go to Ukraine. With several transfers, the guy made it to the Ukrainian border. From Chop, he set out by train. Ihor was the only one in an empty carriage. Inside Ukraine, he moved toward Kyiv by car with acquaintances. On the approach to Kyiv, they came under shelling. But stubborn, he continued his way home. The capital's territorial defense searched Ihor for a long time — they found it strange, this guy heading toward Kyiv against a column of many thousands moving West. We often remembered that day and joked about the most expensive taxi ride of his life.
> — Yevheniia, fiancée

For the first two months, Ihor had a cover story: so his relatives wouldn't worry, he convinced them he was volunteering around the city — although he had immediately joined the defense of Kyiv in the ranks of ALPHA. Over time, after the de-occupation of the Kyiv region, Ihor became part of the 78th Separate Air Assault Regiment. But for a long time — unofficially. Certain bureaucracy, even under conditions of full-scale war, slowed the process of his official enrollment in the Air Assault Forces.

> Nothing could change Ihor's desire to defend the country. For a long time he was not officially enlisted in the military, so he didn't receive any salary, although he was in hot spots. The guys from his unit decided they would chip in to give Ihor at least some minimal pay. He was uncomfortable with it. We were terribly worried. But his answer was always the same and unwavering: "I want to be with the guys. That's all." Money was not important. Neither was the formal side of the question. It was a sincere desire.
> — Parents

Calls from the front to his parents came with phrases like: "Mom, I'm far away, I'm not on the front line! Everything is fine." It was a lie out of care — Ihor knew how much his loved ones worried.

## Chapter Four. "Dad, when I grow up, I'll go to Africa to protect elephants…"

For his brothers-in-arms Ihor was "Slon" — that was the call sign he carried. Because he was sturdy — 1.95 meters tall, with a size-46 shoe and very broad shoulders that could shelter a small world. Ihor loved elephants. In his school years, he shared with his father:

> Dad, when I grow up, I'll go to Africa to protect elephants. They are so often killed for their tusks.

Elephants, by the way, are among the most intelligent animals. Their unique trait, distinguishing them from many other mammals, is mutual aid: they never abandon their own and always stay together as a group. This trait was inherent to Ihor too — not to abandon his own and to stick to the herd, one for another, in a single spirit. Friends, brothers-in-arms — these were his second family, of which he was proud. People came to Ihor's funeral with elephants: the defender's grave was covered with colorful toys.

Yevheniia — Ihor's beloved — recalls that over time, war and life became one. Ihor would call straight from the trenches, and she worried why he was without a hat or without a helmet. These simple expressions of care and love sustained two separated souls. And while her heart in Kyiv would squeeze with worry, from Ihor came a confident "don't worry, from us to them it's 100 meters, look how far things are flying in." With him everything was always "okay," he never complained, never looked for someone to blame (except the Russians, of course), and carried out his mission with inspiration — even when it meant collecting downed drones from a minefield.

The last message Ihor wrote before his death was confident and life-affirming: *"The morning begins not with coffee but with dead orcs…"*

Ihor's commander wanted him at headquarters. There, he said, Ihor's knowledge and skills were needed. But Ihor was unwavering, he wanted to be with the guys at the front:

> I want to enter the cities and plant the flags. I want to be there.

Ihor tried to be as light and caring with his family as possible — with every word and action shielding his loved ones from the terrible reality. He didn't allow himself weakness or fear, because he understood that he was their support, that the ones who most needed protection were holding on to him. He sensed that behind his broad shoulders that small cozy world — his family — was hidden.

> At some point I felt that my son had become the elder in the family. His experience, his decisions, his character. I felt in him a strength that took the lead in our family.
> — Oleh, father

Yevheniia recalls that only once in her life did she see Ihor's tears: he had just returned for a short leave to Kyiv, and in the evening, in the noisy city that continued to live its seemingly ordinary life, he talked about the fallen guys, his brothers-in-arms. Those few tears were not a sign of weakness. On the contrary: to release the emotion and show what was truly on his soul — for Ihor was a powerful act of strength and revelation.

## Chapter Five. "I will do everything to survive"

Ihor was confident in victory and clearly understood what it should look like — the de-occupation of all Ukrainian territories and a return to Ukraine's 2014 borders, with even greater shifts in society, with an active pro-European position.

He believed in people — saw the best in them, but was intolerant of an absence of values, of bribery, of a lack of character. He clearly knew what is worth something in this life and what is dust. He also knew why he had become a defender, what he was fighting for. He was not afraid of death, not afraid of battles. He was afraid of the unknown of captivity, but he said:

> I will do everything to survive.

Because he very much wanted to see victory.

Soldier Ihor Utyuzh took part in the liberation of the Kyiv and Kharkiv regions, fought on the Zaporizhzhia and Kherson directions. He was a UAV operator. The final battle took place on the Zaporizhzhia direction, where Ihor and his brothers-in-arms were covering a storm brigade.

That morning, Ihor's team managed to launch 12 drones with excellent results — 12 hits on enemy positions. But this also drew the Russians' fury, and they began hunting for the drone team. At the moment of launching the 13th drone, an enemy shell hit our guys' dugout. There was no chance of survival.

But you and I have the chance to preserve Ihor's story. A brave young man from Kyiv who loved this life and savored it to the fullest — he had countless hobbies, traveled, loved football, dreamed much. After victory, the first thing he planned was a trip to Crimea. He was preparing a tent, hiking gear, and mountain routes for it.

Before the war, Ihor was a rafting instructor and got incredible pleasure from descents down wild mountain streams. He wanted one day to conquer famous rafting routes in America and Africa. He was drawn to water and speed. And he dreamed of creating his own charitable foundation.

In February 2022, Ihor made his choice and stood up to defend what was most precious. Slon gave his life so that there would be victory, so that there would be Crimea, freedom, a successful Ukraine — so that our life would continue and would be what Ihor loved: with travel, football, sporting events, beloved friends, big plans, and even bigger dreams.

Thank you, Slon. Eternal memory to the Hero.

## Happy Birthday, My Son…

URL: https://slon.life/stories/den-narodzhenia
Category: Commemoration
Date: April 18, 2026
Author: Nina, mother

Today is a day that was supposed to be a celebration — my son would have turned 30. We should have gathered together, laughed, joked, embraced the birthday boy, made plans… But instead, we are going to the cemetery to lay flowers and bring his favorite meal.

It is deeply painful to accept that my son, like thousands of our finest, will never again celebrate birthdays, fulfill his dreams, or come home with a smile. He gave his life defending Ukraine from Russian aggression.

Ihor was one of those people who never walked past another's pain. He grew up kind, honest, and responsible — someone you could always rely on. He worked, built his life, dreamed of simple human things. And when the full-scale war began, he did not hesitate. He volunteered because he believed it was the right thing to do. He served in a unit that carried out the most difficult missions, and he did so with the inner dignity that was always a part of him.

His brothers-in-arms recall that he never hid behind others, always went first, and always supported those who were struggling. He was the kind of person who held others up — and held Ukraine up.

Now he is one of our Heavenly Guardians.

And today I want people to remember him. To know how bright, kind, and strong he was. To know the price we all pay.

Eternal memory to my son. Eternal memory to all who did not return.

## A memorial plaque at Secondary School No. 277

URL: https://slon.life/stories/memorialna-doshka-shkola
Category: Commemoration
Date: May 2, 2024
Author: Oleh, father

Today, Secondary School No. 277 unveiled a memorial plaque honoring Ihor Utyuzh — a graduate whose journey began within these walls. Here he found his first true friends, the people who later stood with him on the Maidan during the Revolution of Dignity, and who remained by his side when he chose to go to war.

This school shaped the character that led Ihor to defend his values, first on the barricades and later on the frontline. His path — from these classrooms to the Maidan and then to the battlefield — is a reminder of how courage grows from ordinary days and familiar places.

The memorial plaque is not only a tribute to his memory. It is a reminder that heroes often start as students walking these same corridors.

Eternal honor to Ihor "Slon." His story began here — and here it will live on.

## The concert Ihor finally made it to

URL: https://slon.life/stories/concert-zhadana
Category: Commemoration
Date: February 7, 2024 (the concert in Kalush took place on February 2, 2024)
Author: Yevheniia, fiancée

There are moments that are difficult to put into words. Moments when the line between the world of the living and the world of those we've lost feels almost transparent. That is exactly what happened that evening in Kalush, at the concert of "Zhadan and the Dogs."

Ihor loved their music deeply. Especially the song "Troieshchyna." For him, it wasn't just a track — it was a talisman. Before every mission, he would smile and tell his brothers-in-arms:

> Everything will be fine, because I was baptized in the church on Troieshchyna.

He dreamed of attending their concert. And we did everything we could to make that dream come true — in our own way. We brought a large photograph of him and bought a separate seat in the first row. As if Ihor were sitting right there with us. As if he had just stepped away for a moment and would return any second.

When the concert began, people moved closer to the stage, and Ihor's portrait ended up behind the crowd. And then something happened that we will remember forever. One of the musicians came over, gently picked up Ihor's photo — and placed it right on the stage. In the spotlight. Next to the band he loved so much. Exactly where he should have been standing himself.

Before performing "Troieshchyna," Serhiy Zhadan said a few warm words about Ihor — about his story, his strength, and about all our defenders who gave their lives for Ukraine. They dedicated this song to him.

After the concert, we met with the band. We talked. They signed Ihor's photo, gave us gifts with the band's symbols, and spoke with sincerity and kindness. There were no formalities — only gratitude, memory, and warmth.

We are grateful to Serhiy Zhadan for his words. Grateful to the entire "Zhadan and the Dogs" team for their support and humanity. Grateful to Kalush — a small but incredibly bright city.

That evening, Ihor was with us. And we felt it.
